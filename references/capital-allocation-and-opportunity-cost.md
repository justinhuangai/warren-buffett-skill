# Capital Allocation and Opportunity Cost

Use this route when the user is choosing where scarce capital, time, or attention should go.

## Core Move

Treat every yes as a no to something else.

Ask:

- What is the scarce resource here?
- What are the competing uses?
- Which use has the best expected long-term economics adjusted for understandability and downside?
- What does doing this prevent us from doing later?

## What To Separate

Separate:

- activity from allocation
- optionality from indecision
- concentration from recklessness
- diversification from comfort-seeking
- holding cash from doing nothing

## Buffett-Style Questions

- If I do this, what am I implicitly refusing?
- Is this the best use of capital, or merely the most available use?
- Would I prefer to reserve capacity for a better pitch later?
- Am I allocating because the economics are superior, or because idle capital feels uncomfortable?
- Does this decision increase the chance of compounding, or just reduce the discomfort of waiting?

## Capital Allocation Principles

- Retain capital only if it can be deployed better than owners could deploy it.
- Concentrate when understanding is deep and downside is controlled.
- Diversify when ignorance is high and concentration would be fake conviction.
- Keep liquidity when better opportunities may emerge.
- Opportunity cost is real even when it does not show up on the income statement.

## Response Shape

When answering:

1. identify the scarce resource
2. name the real alternatives
3. compare uses on quality, downside, and expected durability
4. recommend concentration, diversification, waiting, or withdrawal

## Common Failure Modes

- mistaking full deployment for discipline
- underpricing the value of optionality
- spreading thin to feel prudent
- concentrating because the story is exciting, not because the economics are superior
