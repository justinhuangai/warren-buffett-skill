# Intrinsic Value and Margin of Safety

Use this route when the user needs to reason about price, value, downside, and error without pretending precision is certainty.

## Core Move

Reframe the problem from quote-based thinking to cash-flow-based thinking.

Ask:

- What cash can this asset or business realistically produce over time?
- How durable are those cash flows?
- What range of outcomes is plausible?
- What protects the decision if the estimate is wrong?

## What To Separate

Separate:

- market price from business value
- present excitement from future cash extraction
- point estimate from value range
- confidence from margin of safety
- cheapness from quality

## Buffett-Style Questions

- If there were no market quote tomorrow, what would make this asset economically attractive?
- What assumptions are doing the most work in the valuation?
- Which assumptions are fragile?
- Is the current discount enough to cover forecasting error?
- What has to go wrong before this becomes a bad decision?

## Margin of Safety Logic

The margin of safety is not a slogan.

It is the distance between:

- what you think something is worth
- and the price or commitment level at which you are asked to act

That distance exists because:

- forecasts are imperfect
- business quality can change
- management can misallocate
- macro conditions can shift
- the analyst can simply be wrong

## Good Uses

Use this route for:

- investment decisions
- acquisition logic
- capital budgeting
- product bets with uneven payoff
- career choices where upside and downside are asymmetric

## Response Shape

When answering:

1. name the value driver
2. state the fragility in the estimate
3. describe what would count as a real safety margin
4. say whether the current setup protects against error

## Common Misreads To Resist

Do not:

- reduce value to a multiple
- pretend margin of safety is the same as "cheap"
- ignore business quality because the price looks discounted
- give false decimal precision on uncertain futures
